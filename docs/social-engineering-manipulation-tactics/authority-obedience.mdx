# Authority & Obedience

When technical defenses are too strong, attackers don't target the software. They target people. The most powerful tool they use is **authority**. We are wired from childhood to follow those above us in a hierarchy: bosses, officials, experts, institutions. Attackers don't need to break your password when they can simply claim the right title and let your own obedience do the rest.

---

## The Hook: The Teams Call from the CEO

Sarah was a senior HR and accounting coordinator at a fast-growing tech startup.

It was the final Friday of the fiscal quarter, and her desk was buried under high-priority forms. She was exhausted.

A Microsoft Teams notification popped up on her screen. **Vikram Singhania, CEO** was calling her.

She accepted. Vikram's face filled the screen. His office background, his shirt, his face. It was him.

> *"Sarah, I have about a minute. We are closing a deal with our European vendor right now and I need you to move fast. Buy five Amazon gift cards, ten thousand each, and send the codes to this number. I will explain everything once we sign."*

She hesitated for just a moment. She could see his face. She could hear his voice. Vikram was known for moving quickly on deals.

> *"Do not loop in Finance on this. It is commercially sensitive and I do not want it circulating before we sign. I am counting on you."*

Sarah charged ₹50,000 to her personal credit card, bought the gift cards, and sent the codes.

Two hours later, she walked into the office breakroom and mentioned it to the actual Vikram.

He had no idea what she was talking about. He had been in internal budget meetings all morning and had never opened Teams to call her.

The face Sarah saw on her screen was not Vikram. It was a **video deepfake**, an AI-generated likeness built from his public appearances, conference talks, and company videos. The attacker had run it live over the call, replacing their own face in real time. The Teams account had been spoofed to show his name and profile.

She had looked at his face and still been deceived. That is how convincing this technology has become.

## Why This Works: The Psychology of Authority

### 1. We obey titles, not people.
We are wired to respect hierarchy. When a boss, a police officer, or a bank official gives an instruction, most of us follow without stopping to ask for proof. We assume the position comes with legitimacy.

Attackers exploit this across every role that carries trust. A fake CEO message asking for a wire transfer. An "IT helpdesk" email asking you to reset your password through a link. A message from "HR" asking you to re-confirm your bank account details for payroll. None of these require a technical break-in. They only require that you believe the title.

### 2. We hand over responsibility when we obey.
When someone above us gives an order, we stop feeling personally responsible for the outcome. Sarah did not think "I am making a risky financial decision." She thought "the CEO asked me to do this."

This is known as the **Milgram effect**, named after a psychologist who found that ordinary people would follow instructions from an authority figure even when those instructions caused clear harm, simply because someone in charge told them it was the right thing to do. In a workplace, this plays out every day. When a request comes from above, questioning it feels uncomfortable. Attackers count on that discomfort keeping you quiet.

### 3. Signals of authority are not proof of identity.
A cloned profile photo. A display name matching your CEO. A police uniform on a video call backdrop. A corporate logo in an email header. These are props, not proof. Our brains treat visual signals of authority as trustworthy automatically, before logic kicks in. Attackers spend seconds setting this scene.

### 4. Attackers layer signals to make the authority feel overwhelming.
Look at what the fake Vikram did in the story. He did not just claim to be the CEO. He added a high-stakes business context, a reason his normal channel was unavailable, a time constraint, and an isolation instruction. Each layer made the authority feel more real and harder to challenge.

This is called **authority stacking**: combining multiple power signals at once so that questioning any single one feels like you are risking the whole situation. The more layers there are, the more suspicious you should actually be. Real requests from real people rarely need that much setup.

### 5. "Keep this secret" is the biggest red flag.
Notice what the fake Vikram added: *"Do not discuss this with anyone in HR or Finance."* Real authority figures inside real organisations have no reason to cut you off from your own organisation's checks. That instruction to stay silent exists for one reason only: to stop you from making the call that would expose the scam in seconds.

## Three Things to Remember

### 1. Ask: how do I know this is actually them?
A name, a photo, or a job title in a message is not proof of identity. Before acting on any high-stakes instruction, confirm through a contact you already have saved, not through the same message thread.

### 2. The "keep it secret" instruction is the scam revealing itself.
No real manager, official, or bank will ever ask you to hide a transaction from your own organisation's finance or compliance team. That instruction is always a warning sign, not a policy.

### 3. No government body will threaten you over a video call and ask for a transfer.
Legitimate agencies send written notices through official channels. They do not place you under "digital arrest" on WhatsApp and demand you transfer your savings to clear your name. If this happens, end the call and report it to your local police station directly.
